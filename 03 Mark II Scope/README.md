Mark II Scope

Expanding a validated embedded foundation into a modular telemetry platform.

⸻

Overview

Mark II represents the second major prototype generation of the Secure Battlefield Wearable (SBW) Program.

While Mark I established the secure embedded data pipeline, Mark II focuses on expanding that validated foundation into a more capable and modular telemetry platform.

Rather than replacing the architecture developed during Mark I, Mark II extends it through additional sensing, communications, power management, and system-awareness capabilities while preserving the validation-first philosophy established throughout the program.

The objective of Mark II is not maximum capability—it is controlled architectural growth.

⸻

Mission

The mission of Mark II is to demonstrate that the secure embedded foundation established during Mark I can successfully scale into a modular, multi-sensor platform without sacrificing system integrity, maintainability, or architectural consistency.

Mark II serves as the bridge between a foundational embedded prototype and the more representative hardware platforms planned for future prototype generations.

⸻

Objectives

Mark II expands the prototype by validating several new architectural concepts simultaneously.

Primary objectives include:

* Expand the platform into a modular multi-sensor architecture.
* Validate additional environmental and operational sensing.
* Introduce external communications capabilities.
* Expand system health monitoring.
* Develop broader fault detection and reporting concepts.
* Introduce dedicated power subsystem validation.
* Continue refinement of host tooling and system interpretation.
* Preserve compatibility with the validated architecture established during Mark I.

Each objective is validated independently before becoming part of the broader platform.

⸻

Hardware Expansion

While Mark I intentionally minimized hardware complexity, Mark II introduces several new prototype subsystems that significantly expand platform capability.

Major additions include:

Weapon Awareness

Additional sensing is introduced to provide greater visibility into weapon state and operating conditions.

Examples include:

* Barrel temperature sensing
* Fire selector sensing
* Expanded operational state awareness

⸻

Position Awareness

Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) capability is introduced to investigate how spatial context may contribute to future telemetry applications.

Initial validation focuses on integrating positional information into the broader telemetry architecture rather than developing navigation functionality.

⸻

Communications

Mark II introduces external communications hardware to begin validating modular transport concepts and future connectivity options.

This stage focuses on demonstrating architectural flexibility rather than continuous communication or networked operation.

⸻

Identity & Context

RFID technologies are introduced to explore future concepts involving identification, inventory management, and contextual awareness.

These capabilities serve as foundational building blocks for future ecosystem expansion.

⸻

Power Architecture

Dedicated battery hardware and power management subsystems are introduced to begin validating portable operation and system power awareness.

Mark II investigates how power state, system health, and runtime behavior interact within a modular embedded platform.

⸻

New Capabilities

Building upon the secure embedded foundation established during Mark I, Mark II investigates several additional architectural capabilities.

These include:

* Multi-sensor data collection
* Expanded system health awareness
* Fault observation and reporting
* Modular communications
* Position awareness
* Identity awareness
* Improved runtime flexibility
* Expanded external tooling support

Each capability contributes to a broader understanding of how future telemetry systems may evolve while remaining grounded in validation-first engineering.

⸻

Validation Goals

Every subsystem introduced during Mark II exists to answer specific engineering questions.

Key validation objectives include:

* Can additional sensors be integrated while maintaining architectural consistency?
* Can multiple information sources coexist within a unified telemetry platform?
* Can communications be introduced without compromising the validated embedded foundation?
* Can runtime awareness expand as system complexity increases?
* Can power management become an observable subsystem rather than simply a supporting utility?

Successful validation provides confidence that future prototype generations can continue expanding the platform while preserving system reliability.

⸻

Architectural Evolution

Mark II represents the transition from a proof-of-concept embedded device toward a modular telemetry architecture.

Where Mark I focused on validating a secure embedded pipeline, Mark II begins validating how independent subsystems cooperate within a larger platform.

This progression establishes the architectural foundation necessary for future custom hardware, broader ecosystem integration, and increasingly representative prototype systems.

⸻

Looking Ahead

Mark II is not intended to represent the final SBW platform.

Instead, it serves as the architectural bridge between foundational embedded validation and future prototype generations.

Lessons learned throughout Mark II directly influence the design of Mark III, where the program begins transitioning toward custom hardware, improved mechanical integration, and more representative prototype systems.

By validating architectural growth before pursuing additional complexity, the SBW Program continues following its guiding philosophy of incremental engineering, measured risk reduction, and long-term system evolution.

Current efforts focus on:

As development progresses, this document will continue evolving alongside the program while maintaining a high-level architectural perspective suitable for public release.
